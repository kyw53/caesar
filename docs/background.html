Background Information

Gallic Wars

Gaius Julius Caesar was already entrenched in the world of politics by the time he won his consulship in 59 BC. Earning popular support through policies like land reform, he then became governor of the Gallic provinces of Rome. The Gauls were a loosely confederated group of continental Celts who had a long history of resisting assimilation by the Romans, shown through events like the Sack of Rome in 390 BCE. When a subgroup of Gauls, the Helvetii, migrated west and toward the Roman provinces, Caesar took it as an act of aggression and began his assault on the Gallic homelands.

Civil War

The conquest of Gaul led to numerous debates in the Senate. On one hand, Caesar held popular sway by using plunder from the Gallic Wars to reward his soldiers, fund massive public works like the Forum Iulium, and provide debt relief. But politicians rooted in the principals of the Republic, like Cato the Younger, labelled the campaign as a war of expansion that would only enrich Caesar, who was now powerful enough to declare himself monarch and march on Rome. The tension burst when the Senate, headed by Caesar's former friend Pompey, forced tribune Mark Antony to flee the city and declared Caesar an enemy of the state. Thinking he had no other choice, Caesar gathered his men and crossed the Rubicon.
